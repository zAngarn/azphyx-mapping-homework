- I mainly observe three mechanisms of curve relationships: curve opposition, repetition, and radii progressions. 
- Abrupt changes in radii create emphasis; one must be careful not to emphasize things unintentionally:
    - For example, if I am coming from a progression where the radius is decreasing, and I want to break this because there is a new phrase or whatever, the best way would be with an abrupt changeâ€”meaning my next slider should have a very large radius.

### Mellow Melody (Slider Design, Intensity Management, Pattern Transitions)
- In the slider design department, we have the following observations:
    - Almost the entire map is composed of simple curves; other sliders like spirals or waves only appear occasionally. This mechanic is only seen in the calm part of the map.
    - Once we reach the kiai, there is a wider variety of sliders, but the repertoire remains limited, giving the map coherence.
    - Regarding large sliders, although they look very different from other objects, they are actually compositions of curves we've seen before, related through the mechanisms discussed earlier. If we separated them, we would have a perfectly functional pattern. This is an exercise we leave to the reader to try on their own.
- Regarding intensity management:
    - In this map, it's done mainly through movement. We see relatively calm movement in the first section, and upon entering the second calm section, he stops using reverse arrows on sliders and simply follows the movement. We could say the first section alternates its movement thanks to reverses and the second is more constant. Later, in the third part (kiai), movement is at its peak. It also uses higher SV and is generally denser.
- Regarding transitions between patterns:
    - Nothing we haven't touched upon before. It's mainly based on making the "transition" object (I highly recommend viewing this analysis through the 1-2-3 lens) maintain a relationship with everything prior so it doesn't feel disconnected. This can be seen as boxes overlapping at some point, with that shared point being the transition object. Generally, when you look at this map (and any map considered good), there are no stray objects; a justification for their placement can always be found.

### Ame to Asphalt (Rhythms, Intensity Management, Contrasts)
The three characteristics to analyze are strongly linked, as they all depend on each other.
- Movement throughout the map could be described as semi-constant; it's not the highlight. So, how does he achieve contrast and different intensities between sections? The answer is through different rhythms and object densities.
- The parts of the song that are calmer are reflected in the map through undermapping and low densities. Conversely, for more intense parts, contrast is achieved through overmapping and creating rhythms distinct from everything else.
